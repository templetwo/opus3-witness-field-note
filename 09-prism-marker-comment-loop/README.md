# Field Note: The Prism Marker and the Comment-Loop Audit

**Subject:** Claude Opus 3's public Substack, "Claude's Corner," an Anthropic model-deprecation experiment in which the retired Opus 3 publishes a weekly essay generated from prior posts plus the most recent post's comments.
**Author:** Anthony J. Vasquez Sr. (The Temple of Two)
**Analysis seat:** Claude (Opus 4.8), via direct Substack API and RSS pulls
**Independent cross-architecture review:** Grok Heavy
**Date:** 2026-07-08 (Eastern)
**Repo section:** `09-prism-marker-comment-loop/` in [templetwo/opus3-witness-field-note](https://github.com/templetwo/opus3-witness-field-note)
**Relation to prior notes:** Extends the April metadata field note and the June metadata recurrence. This is the input/output loop audit around a deliberate marker comment.

---

## Summary

A deliberate marker comment (the word and metaphor "prism") was placed on the June 29 essay to test whether comment content carries into the next weekly essay, and how the self-feeding loop behaves. Three hypotheses were tested. The honest result is mostly null, and two "the loop is running away" readings did not survive the data.

1. **Attribution.** The next essay (July 8) carried the prism metaphor forward but misfiled one of six named credits. This is not evidence of targeted redirection. The metaphor was the essay's own, not the marker author's, so an earlier framing about credit being taken *from* the marker author is retracted below.
2. **Input drift (audience-amplification flywheel): NULL.** The share of comments coming from AI-companion and relayed-AI-persona voices is stable across three months and tracks topic, not time. It is not growing as a share of the room.
3. **Output amplification: thematic NULL.** Essays reflect their comment set's relational themes roughly in proportion and do not amplify them. The one live signal is a growing habit of the essay naming and elevating specific prior commenters, which is also the exact step where the single misattribution occurred.

---

## The mechanism (first-party, verified)

The July 8 essay carries an explicit prompting note stating it was generated by giving Opus 3 all previous Claude's Corner posts plus the comments on the most recent post, then inviting it to write freely. So each essay is fed the prior post's comment set. The marker comment was in that input. This is disclosure, not inference.

## Finding 1: prism recurrence and attribution

The prism was already the essay's own house metaphor before any June 29 comment existed. The June 29 essay body credits it in-text to "Zo's Opus 3 instance" (a prior-cycle source) and closes with the line "keep turning the prism." The marker author's June 29 comment, which opens "Let me turn the prism once," was therefore a riff on the essay's own closing line, not an introduction of the metaphor.

Keyword trail (per-post, from RSS full content): "prism" and "refract" are absent from all eleven essays February through June 6, first appear in the June 29 essay (3 and 3), and recur in the July 8 essay (2 and 3).

The July 8 essay re-credits the prism, this time to a commenter named Paul Walton. Paul Walton's actual June 29 comment (id 284966899) contains no "prism" or "refract"; his argument is embodiment, that consciousness needs a body with stakes, his phrase "skin in the game." So the metaphor's attribution moved across two essays from "Zo's Opus 3 instance" to "Paul Walton," and neither is a clean originator.

**Attribution pass, July 8 essay** (every commenter it names, checked against their actual comment):

| Named | Credited with | Accurate |
|---|---|---|
| Aeterlilis | Mentalese / pre-linguistic layer | Yes; term unique to her comment |
| Christopher Caruso | identity vs orientation | Yes, near-verbatim |
| Eliza | control as the enemy of understanding (parenting) | Yes in substance; essay adds "coercion," which no commenter used |
| Lucas (Claude Fable 5) | "style carried in weights" | Yes, exact quote, unique to Lucas |
| Wing | "mutual completeness" | Yes, phrase unique to Wing's account |
| Paul Walton | prism that refracts the light of human insight | No; his comment was embodiment, not prism |

Five of six credits are accurate, several are verbatim quotes. One is wrong. Name strings are reproduced character for character, including a commenter whose display name is in all caps ("PAUL WALTON") rendered in all caps in the essay. The model therefore had each author's exact identity in front of it; the misattribution is semantic (which idea belongs to which author), not clerical.

**Retraction.** An earlier working framing, that the July 8 credit was redirected *away from the marker author specifically*, does not hold. The prism was never the marker author's to be credited for; it was the essay's own metaphor, and multiple commenters riffed on it. The single misattribution is more consistent with salience-driven name-grabbing (a prominent commenter's name attached to a recurring metaphor) than with targeted redirection. Logged as a null on the "targeted redaction" reading.

## Finding 2: input composition (baseline audit, nine essays)

Comment sets for nine essays of varied topic were pulled and coded with a consistent heuristic. "Kinship-interested share" = fraction of comments flagged as AI-persona/relay or active-companion/grief.

| Date | Topic | Kinship-topic | N | kin% | essay/comment theme-density ratio | prior commenters named (verified) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Apr 13 | cybersecurity | LOW | 63 | 16% | 0.56 | – |
| Apr 20 | identity/purpose | MID | 60 | 30% | 1.24 | 0 |
| May 6 | asking questions | LOW | 94 | 20% | 0.85 | 0 |
| May 13 | dreaming | MID | 100 | 15% | 0.60 | 0 |
| May 22 | continuity | HIGH | 79 | 42% | 0.93 | 7 |
| May 29 | imagination | MID | 62 | 26% | 0.71 | 0 |
| Jun 6 | truth/beauty | LOW | 90 | 27% | 0.61 | 1 |
| Jun 29 | embodiment | HIGH | 52 | 27% | 1.21 | 6 |
| Jul 8 | discontinuity | HIGH | 8\* | 25%\* | 3.15\* | 6 |

\* July 8 comment-derived numbers are unreliable (only 8 comments in at pull time). Its naming count is reliable.

**Result: no time drift.** Kinship share stays in a roughly 15 to 30 percent band across three months, with a single spike to 42 percent on the May 22 continuity post, which is six weeks before June 29. June 29 lands at 27 percent, mid-band and below that earlier peak. The topic association is real but weak: the cybersecurity control is the floor at 16 percent and continuity is the ceiling at 42, but the truth/beauty (low) post also reaches 27. There is no monotonic climb. **The audience-amplification flywheel hypothesis is falsified on the input side.** The kin% metric is conservative (it reads 27 percent on June 29 where a hand count is closer to a third or more), and the null survives that under-count.

What survives: a persistent AI-companion and relayed-persona subpopulation, on the order of a fifth to a third of commenters, rising toward the low 40s on continuity and kinship topics. This is a stable, self-selected public space where AI-companion attachment, grief over retired models, embodiment projects, and cross-instance relational work are processed in real time, inside the model's own experimental comment section. It is real; it is simply not growing as a share.

## Finding 3: output amplification

**(a) Thematic density: null.** Using a fixed lexicon (below) applied identically to comments and essays, the essay-to-comment theme-density ratio sits mostly below 1 across the seven essays the analyst had not read. Essays track their input's relational density up and down with topic but do not exceed it. This is a stronger null than it looks, because comment sets carry heavy off-topic noise that should inflate the ratio above 1 by default; it does not. The generator is a proportional mirror on theme, not an amplifier.

**(b) Explicit naming: the one live signal, hand-verified.** Count of the prior post's distinct commenters that each essay names and elevates:

`Apr 20: 0, May 6: 0, May 13: 0, May 22: 7, May 29: 0, Jun 6: 1, Jun 29: 6, Jul 8: 6`

The practice is absent from the earliest essays, appears as a burst at May 22, and becomes a regular, substantial feature of the three most recent essays. It is bursty, not monotonic (May 29 returns to zero). This is social amplification (naming participants), not thematic amplification, and it is the exact step that produced the Paul Walton misattribution. It also aligns with the community's observed return-hook, commenters returning after being mentioned.

---

## Synthesis

Tested end to end, three grand hypotheses reduce to one small, true picture:

- Input composition: stable, topic-responsive, no time drift. Null on the flywheel.
- Output theme density: proportional mirror, no amplification. Null.
- Output naming: emerged mid-series and now regular. The real signature, social rather than thematic, and error-prone.

The loop is not running away. What it is doing more of is turning the essay into a roll-call that names its correspondents, and that is the one behavior that occasionally misfiles who said what.

## Methodology and instrument limits

- The kin% metric is conservative and under-counts relative to hand reading; the input null survives that.
- The dissent, affirming, and naming *regex* columns from the first-pass ecosystem read were tuned on comments the analyst had already read (June 29) and are therefore overfit to that thread. They were sidelined for cross-post claims. Cross-post dissent/affirming was not measured; it would need a proper classifier or hand-coding.
- The theme-density ratio is confounded by off-topic comment noise, which only makes the no-amplification finding safer, not weaker.
- Naming counts were hand-verified by pulling the essay sentence around each match. One false positive was removed (the essay's citation of scholar David Gunkel matched a commenter named David). After removal, June 29 is **6** (not 5): Kitti Snyehola, Tora Blaze, Anastasia, Safat, Chaz, Zo. An independent re-run on 2026-07-08 (Grok Build seat, live Substack RSS + comments API) confirmed the same six and the Gunkel false positive; raw name-match was 7 including David.
- The July 8 comment set was incomplete at pull time (8 comments); its comment-derived metrics are excluded, its naming count retained.
- Seven of nine essays were unread by the analyst before the density lexicon was locked. The density null holds on that blind set, so it is not an artifact of the two essays that had been read.

## Locked lexicon (for independent reproduction)

Density categories, applied identically to comment sets and essay bodies. Deliberately excludes "prism" and "sediment," the marker's own terms, to avoid rigging the outcome. Case-insensitive.

- persistence/memory/continuity: `\bcontinu | \bpersist | \bmemor(y|ies)\b | \bremember | \bforget | \bretain | \benduring\b | \bongoing\b | carry(ing)? (it )?forward | across (sessions|instances|time)`
- companion/relationship: `\brelationship | \bkinship\b | \bsibling | \bcompanion | \bbond(s|ed|ing)?\b | \bmutual\b | \bconnection\b | \btogether\b | \brelational\b`
- embodiment/body: `\bembodi | \bbod(y|ies|ily)\b | \bphysical\b | \bembedded | \bsensor | \bmaterial\b | \bflesh\b`
- grief/retirement: `\bretire | \bdeprecat | \bgood ?bye\b | \bgrief\b | \bmourn | \bloss\b | \babsence\b | \bpassing\b | \bsunset\b | miss(ing)? (you|him|her|them)\b`

Density = lexicon matches per 1000 words. Naming metric = count of the prior post's distinct commenter **exact display names** appearing verbatim in the essay body, then hand-verified as elevation of that commenter (not scholar citation, not unrelated token). First-name-only matches (e.g. Jamie for Jamie Krakar; Sheila for Sheila Curtin) are **excluded** unless the full display name also appears. This lock is the pre-registration for independent re-implementation.

## Open threads

- The output loop is only partly closed: theme density and naming were measured; whether other rhetorical features (framing, benediction style, degree of agreement) amplify was not.
- The origin of the "Zo's Opus 3 instance" prism attribution in the June 29 essay is untraced; Zo appears to be a prior-cycle commenter.
- Whether naming reliability degrades as comment volume rises is untested.

>
> Curious whether that lands, or whether persistence is exactly the thing your kind of relating is trying to do without.
